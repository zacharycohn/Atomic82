Life is complicated.

Testing your drinking water for lead shouldn't be.

Your drinking water is unique to your home

Your local water utility does tests at the water treatment plants. But where does your water go after that?

  • through water mains buried under the streets
  • into the water pipes under your house
  • through your kitchen pipes
  • Finally, out of your faucet or refrigerator.

Your local government does only has to test a small number of homes in your city, and they don't necessarily do it every year.


What does this mean?

The water your family drinks and cooks with gets to you through a unique path. Each step involves pipes and connection points installed and managed by different companies and people, at different times with different laws.

Why you need to test for lead

Flint, Michigan opened our eyes

Many people know about the lead contamination crisis happening in Flint, Michigan. But many people don’t realize that aging infrastructure and lax regulatory systems are a nation-wide crisis.

Water issues are a growing conversation in communities around the country.

Houses built pre-1986 used lead pipes

Lead pipes used to be very common in cities and houses before we knew how dangerous they are. They were banned in 1986, but that law didn't require replacing the lead pipes already out there.

Unfortunately, many cities didn't keep great records. Many aren't sure if they have lead pipes, or if they do, where they even are.

You can't see or taste lead

Your water may seem normal, but you can't see or taste lead in water. Unlike other contaminents, it won't be a weird color, smell strange, or taste funny.

And yet, as it builds up in your body over time, it can hinders brain function, stunts growth in children, and have other long-term health implications.

Children younger than 6 years are especially vulnerable to lead poisoning, which can severely affect mental and physical development. At very high levels, lead poisoning can be fatal.

We partner with professional labs

We don't use the unreliable testing strips you can find on Amazon or at your department store. We send your water sample to our professional, government-certified drinking water testing laboratory partners.

Our labs test your water for lead using advanced, state of the art testing equipment called inductively coupled plasma mass spectrometry to determine what is in your water - and how much.

The EPA regulatory threshold for taking drastic action is 15 parts per billion.

However, this is a bit decieving: The CDC, pediatricians and health professionals, and even the EPA all say there is NO safe amount of lead.

Even a little bit of lead can build up over time and be harmful, particularly to growing kids and people with health concerns.

The chemists we work with can find trace amounts of lead down to 1 part per billion. Since there is no such thing as a safe level of lead, we think this level of specificity matters.
